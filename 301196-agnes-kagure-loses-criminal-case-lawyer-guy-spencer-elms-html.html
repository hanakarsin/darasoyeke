Agnes Kagure loses criminal case against lawyer Guy Spencer Elms

Posted by Chauncey Koziol on Wednesday, August 21, 2024

- The case which has dragged on since 2017 was dismissed on Wednesday, March 20, by magistrate Joseline Onyango

- Kagure had filed a criminal case against the lawyer for forging the deceased owner's will to lay claim on the property

- The parcels of land in question belonged to the late Roger Bryan Robson who died in August 2012

- With no wife and children, Robson left the will to be executed by his lawyer Guy Spencer who was to sell and proceeds donated to environmental initiatives

Businesswoman Agnes Kagure has suffered a blow in her legal quest to have a KSh 500 million Karen property after a court threw out her criminal case against lawyer Guy Spencer Elms.

The former Nairobi deputy governor nominee had accused the lawyer of forging documents making him the property owner’s sole will executioner.

The case which has dragged on since 2017 was dismissed on Wednesday, March 20, by magistrate Joseline Onyango over insufficient evidence to sustain a criminal case.

The Director of Public Prosecution’s initial application to have the case withdrawn was challenged by Kagure’s lawyer who accused the DPP’s office of not giving proper reasons.

The latest development means the former insurance broker might eventually lose the civil case in which her controversial ownership of the Karen property is being challenged.

“With the false accusation of forgery behind us, we are now hopeful the other related matters will be expended with fairly quickly and my client can finally execute the will for which he was rightfully appointed executor,’ said Guys's lawyer Oscar Litoro after the ruling.

Court documents of the civil case regarding the properties showed Kagure claimed to have bought the land from the original owner, Rodger Brayn Robson, who died in 1997 at KSh 100 million.

“She forged the deceased’s signatures on the land titles number 2327/10 and 2327/27 claiming to have purchased it at KSh 100 million. The documents she relied on to evict occupants are forgeries and not recognised in law,” said Guy in court proceedings in 2015.

The court also heard that before he died, Robson appointed the lawyer the sole will executioner of the estate.

It is this claim which resulted in the criminal case which has now been thrown out for lack of proper evidence against Guy.

Robson owned two properties in Kenya, in Karen and another in Upper Hill Nairobi, both estimated to be valued at KSh 500 million.

His wish was for his estate to be sold and proceeds donated to environmental initiatives.
