Judiciary on the spot as Kenyans expose how judges and magistrates report to work late

Posted by Lourie Helzer on Saturday, March 23, 2024

-Members of the public revealed proceedings in some courts have been starting at almost midday

-Others shared pictures of litigants waiting for judges to arrive well past 9am, the official opening time

-The scenario pours cold water on Chief Justice David Maraga’s pledge to address case backlog in courts

-Maraga acknowledged the problem last month and sent a terse memo directing officials to keep time

Gross laxity within the Kenyan Judiciary has been exposed after members of the public joined hands to illustrate how judges and magistrates have been reporting to work well past official court opening hours with impunity.

The scenario, which appears to pour cold water on Chief Justice David Maraga’s pledge to address the massive case backlog in courts, was triggered by Senior Counsel Ahmednasir Abdullahi, who randomly took and shared photos from three courtrooms where judges had not arrived after 9am.

“The Kenyan Judiciary has gone to the dogs...functionally dead...,” he stated on his Twitter account on Wednesday, June 12.

Other Kenyans soon joined in and shared similar situations in other courts across the country.

Some noted that they had to wait until 11am for a magistrate to surface while some described how staff at court registries strolled in to work well past 9am.

“Now I am in Makadara Traffic Court. They indicated that we should be there by 8am but the judge comes at 11am and I’ve been told that it's the way they operate. It is wasting half day waiting for a judge,” said one Mako Koro.“The other day I was at Makadara courts, Court one and magistrate came at 12 noon,” added Karori wa Ng’ethe, alluding to particularly deplorable complacency at the Nairobi station.

The worrying revelations come amid a deep case backlog crisis in the Judiciary, with close to half of the unresolved cases in Kenyan courts having dragged on for more than three years.

Only last month, Chief Registrar of the Judiciary Anne Amadi attributed the crisis to shortage of staff, saying it will take the Judiciary four years to clear the backlog of cases unless more judges and magistrates are hired.

With 674 judicial officers against more than 42 million Kenyans, Amadi said the number of fresh cases filed in the last financial year plus pending ones were overwhelming for the current judicial officers.

In the year under review, 344,130 fresh cases were filed, 258,180 of which were criminal and 85,190 civil.

Only May 2019, Maraga acknowledged that proceedings in some courts were beginning late and sent a memo directing business kick off at 9am.

"In the event that a Judge of Judicial officer is out of the station for whatever reason, it shall be the responsibility of the said Judge of Judicial officer to issue adequate notice to the litigants, advocates and members of the public to avoid unnecessary inconvenience and expense,” read the memo dated May 16, 2019.
